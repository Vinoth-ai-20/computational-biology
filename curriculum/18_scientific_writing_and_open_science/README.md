# Module 18 — Scientific Writing and Open Science

**Depth tier:** Tier 2 — Working competence  
**Week budget:** 4 weeks  
**Notebooks:** 12  

## Motivation

You can build a perfect model and nobody will know. Scientific impact requires
communicating results clearly — in papers, in proposals, in talks, in code
documentation. This module treats writing as a technical skill: learnable,
systematic, and testable. It also covers open science practices (preprints,
FAIR data, reproducibility standards) that are now required — not optional —
by major funders and PhD programmes.

## Depth-tier rationale (Tier 2)

Tier 1 would mean writing and submitting a full research paper — appropriate
only after completing the substantive research in other modules. Tier 2 gives
the working toolkit: you can write a structured, clear draft of a methods section,
abstract, or introduction; produce publication-quality figures; write a PhD
personal statement; and understand peer review from both sides. These outputs are
Track A and Track B deliverables, not side skills.

## Track A relevance

**Why this helps Track A (India RA/JRF/Bioinformatics Engineer):**
Research associate applications require a cover letter, a statement of purpose,
and often a writing sample. Clear scientific communication distinguishes
candidates in interviews — being able to explain your work to a supervisor
concisely and in writing is a primary evaluation criterion. Reproducible code
(NB04) and FAIR data practices (NB08) are increasingly required in Indian
research institutes (DST, DBT, CSIR requirements).

## Track B relevance

**Why this helps Track B (European PhD):**
EMBL, IMPRS, and equivalent programmes require: a personal statement (~1500
words), a research proposal (~500–1500 words), and often a writing sample.
Letters of recommendation describe how you communicate and write. A preprint
or conference abstract is a significant differentiator. NB10 builds the PhD
application package directly.
