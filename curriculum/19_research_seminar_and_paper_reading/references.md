# Module 19 — References

## Core methodological guides

### How to read papers

- **Keshav, S. (2007). "How to Read a Paper."** ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review 37(3):83–84.
  - The definitive source for the Three-Pass Method used throughout this module.
  - DOI: 10.1145/1273445.1273458
  - Free: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1273445.1273458

- **Mensh, B. & Kording, K. (2017). "Ten Simple Rules for Structuring Papers."** PLOS Computational Biology 13(9):e1005619.
  - Complements the reading framework with the author's perspective — understanding how papers are structured helps you read them faster.
  - DOI: 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005619

- **Hubbard, K.E. & Dunbar, S.D. (2017). "Perceptions of scientific research literature and strategies for reading papers depend on academic career stage."** PLOS ONE 12(12):e0189753.
  - Empirical evidence on how reading strategies change with expertise — useful for calibrating where you are.

- **Seibert Hanson, A.E. & Brown, C.M. (2019). "Improving lab meetings and journal clubs by identifying and addressing student fears."** CBE Life Sciences Education 18(3):ar43.
  - Practical guidance for journal club dynamics.

### How to give talks and presentations

- **Bourne, P.E. (2007). "Ten Simple Rules for Making Good Oral Presentations."** PLOS Computational Biology 3(4):e77.
  - DOI: 10.1371/journal.pcbi.0030077

- **Bourne, P.E. & Korngreen, A. (2006). "Ten Simple Rules for Reviewers."** PLOS Computational Biology 2(9):e110.
  - Understanding reviewers' perspectives improves both reading and writing.

- **Erren, T.C. & Bourne, P.E. (2007). "Ten Simple Rules for a Good Poster Presentation."** PLOS Computational Biology 3(5):e102.

### How to write literature reviews

- **Pautasso, M. (2013). "Ten Simple Rules for Writing a Literature Review."** PLOS Computational Biology 9(7):e1003149.
  - DOI: 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1003149

- **Snyder, H. (2019). "Literature review as a research methodology: An overview and guidelines."** Journal of Business Research 104:333–339.
  - Distinguishes narrative, systematic, and scoping reviews clearly.

## Statistical literacy

- **Nuzzo, R. (2014). "Scientific method: Statistical errors."** Nature 506:150–152.
  - Accessible explanation of p-value misinterpretation for a broad scientific audience.

- **Ioannidis, J.P.A. (2005). "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False."** PLOS Medicine 2(8):e124.
  - Foundational paper on statistical power and false discovery in research.

- **Colquhoun, D. (2014). "An investigation of the false discovery rate and the misinterpretation of p-values."** Royal Society Open Science 1:140216.

- **Cohen, J. (1992). "A power primer."** Psychological Bulletin 112(1):155–159.
  - The classic reference for effect size (Cohen's d) and power analysis.

## Reference management tools

- **Zotero** — https://www.zotero.org/
  - Free, open-source reference manager. Browser plugin + desktop app + BibTeX export.
  - Documentation: https://www.zotero.org/support/

- **doi2bib** — https://doi2bib.org/
  - Convert any DOI to a BibTeX entry instantly.

- **Connected Papers** — https://www.connectedpapers.com/
  - Visual literature explorer: builds a graph of papers related to a seed paper. Excellent for finding landmark works and understanding a field's citation network.

- **Semantic Scholar** — https://www.semanticscholar.org/
  - AI-powered search with citation context, influence metrics, and open-access PDF links.

- **Litmaps** — https://www.litmaps.com/
  - Citation map over time; useful for tracing how a field evolved.

## Benchmarking and reproducibility

- **Mangul, S. et al. (2019). "Systematic benchmarking of omics computational tools."** Nature Communications 10:1393.
  - Gold standard for what a good computational biology benchmark looks like.

- **Boulesteix, A.-L. et al. (2013). "A plea for neutral comparison studies in computational sciences."** PLOS ONE 8(4):e61562.
  - Classic critique of biased benchmark design.

- **Gentleman, R. & Lang, D.T. (2007). "Statistical Analyses and Reproducible Research."** Journal of Computational and Graphical Statistics 16(1):1–23.
